Overview

• A gallstone is a deposit of hardened bile that resembles a pebble that can develop in the gallbladder. Just below your liver on the right side of your abdomen is your gallbladder, a little, pear-shaped organ. Bile, a digestive fluid made in your liver and kept in your gallbladder, is stored in and released by the gallbladder. When you eat, your gallbladder contracts and releases bile into your duodenum, which is part of your small intestine.

Symptoms

• Significant abdominal pain in the upper right or middle of the abdomen • Right shoulder or back pain • Vomiting and diarrhea • Fever and Chills • Skin and eye discoloration. • Light-colored faeces and Black urine

Causes

• There is too much cholesterol in your bile. Normally, the molecules in your bile are sufficient to break down the cholesterol your liver excretes. However, if your liver excretes more cholesterol than your bile can break down, it may crystallize and eventually turn into stones. • There is too much bilirubin in your bile. The chemical bilirubin is created when your body destroys red blood cells. Your liver produces too much bilirubin under some circumstances, such as liver cirrhosis, biliary tract infections, and specific blood diseases. Gallstone development is influenced by the excess bilirubin. • Your gallbladder does not properly drain. Bile may become extremely concentrated if your gallbladder doesn't drain fully or frequently enough, which can lead to the development of gallstones.

Risk Factors

• Gallstones are more common in those over the age of 40. • Gallstones are more frequent in females than males. • Do gallstones run in your family? If there is a history of them, you are more likely to acquire them yourself. • Obesity: Obesity is a substantial risk factor for gallstones. • Rapid weight loss: If you lose weight quickly, you are more prone to acquire gallstones. • Diet: A low-fiber, high-cholesterol, high-fat diet may increase your risk of gallstones.

Complications

Common Complications

• Gallbladder Attack: An acute, stabbing pain in the upper right abdomen that might continue for several hours. • Biliary colic: A gallstone that temporarily plugs a bile duct can cause biliary colic, a milder form of discomfort. • Jaundice: When bile backs up into the blood, it can cause a yellowing of the skin and eyes known as jaundice. • Cholecystitis: A gallbladder inflammation, cholecystitis. • An inflammation of the pancreas is referred to as pancreatitis.

Less Common Complications

• Gallbladder cancer: The gallbladder can get infected with this uncommon kind of cancer. • Bile duct infection: If a gallstone plugs a bile duct and bacteria get into the bile, a bile duct infection, a dangerous condition, can result. • Gallstone pancreatitis: A gallstone that blocks the pancreatic duct results in this particular type of pancreatitis.

Prevent/Avoid

• Avoid skipping meals. Try to eat at the same time every day. Fasting or skipping meals both raise the risk of gallstones. • Slowly reduce your weight. Slow down if you need to lose weight. Gallstone risk can increase with rapid weight loss. Aim to lose 1 to 2 pounds (0.5 to 1 kilogram) per week. • Eat more foods high in fiber. Increase your intake of fiber-rich foods like fruits, vegetables, and whole grains. • Keep a healthy weight. Gallstones are more likely to form in people who are overweight and obese. By consuming less calories and engaging in more exercise, you can work toward achieving a healthy weight. Once you reach a healthy weight, attempt to keep it off by sticking to a balanced diet and exercising regularly.

Treatment/Care

• Surgery to remove the gallbladder (cholecystectomy) • Medications to dissolve gallstones. • Laparoscopic cholecystectomy • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP)
